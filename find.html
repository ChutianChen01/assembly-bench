Step 1 — Research

How to find the part you need

Before you assemble, you need DNA sequences. Different databases are good for different things. Here's a beginner-friendly map of where to look. Once you have a sequence, head to Grab the Sequence to get it cleanly into AssemblyBench.

Pub

PubMed

Find papers describing the part

Start here when you have a vague idea ("a fluorescent protein that's redder than mCherry"). Read the papers — they cite the original sequence sources.

Use when: you don't know what gene or organism yet.
G·S

Google Scholar

Broader literature search

Useful for grey literature, preprints, theses, and following citation trails. Pairs well with PubMed for early-stage discovery.

Use when: PubMed feels too narrow.
NCBI

NCBI (GenBank / Gene / Nucleotide)

The canonical DNA sequence source

Search by gene name, accession number, or organism. Most papers reference an NCBI accession — that's where the official DNA sequence lives.

Use when: you know the gene name and want the DNA.
Uni

UniProt

The protein-centric database

Best when you care about the protein (function, domains, structure links). Each UniProt entry links back to its coding DNA on NCBI.

Use when: you're picking a protein to express.
PDB

Protein Data Bank

3D protein structures

If you need to know what your protein looks like (active sites, binding pockets), the PDB is the place. Use it to decide which variant to clone.

Use when: you're engineering a protein, not just expressing it.
Add

Addgene

Real plasmids you can order

A non-profit plasmid repository. Search for a published plasmid, see its full sequence and map, and request it for ~$80. Excellent for backbones.

Use when: you want a known working plasmid as a starting point.
iGEM

iGEM Parts Registry

Community standard parts

Thousands of "BioBrick" parts (promoters, RBSs, reporters) submitted by student teams. Quality varies — check the experience pages.

Use when: you want common parts with prior characterization data.
Bench

Benchling Public

Shared cloning notebooks

Many labs publish their plasmid maps openly on Benchling. Searching here often turns up annotated sequences ready to copy.

Use when: you're hunting for a well-annotated map.
Beginner tip. If you're brand new, this is the typical path: idea → PubMed paper → NCBI accession → FASTA sequence → paste into AssemblyBench. Don't try to memorize all eight resources at once.
