Step 2 — Sequence

How to grab the sequence

Once you know what you want (see Find a Part), here's how to get the raw DNA letters into AssemblyBench without making the classic beginner mistakes.

  1. 1

    Search for your gene or protein

    On NCBI, use the Gene or Nucleotide database. On UniProt, use the search bar with the protein name plus the organism (e.g., GFP Aequorea victoria).

    Pro tip: include the organism. "Insulin" in human is a different sequence from "insulin" in zebrafish.

  2. 2

    Open the right record

    Pick the entry marked reviewed (on UniProt: a gold star) or the most-cited RefSeq (on NCBI: accession starts with NM_, NP_, or NC_).

  3. 3

    Switch the view to FASTA

    On NCBI, click "Send to" → File → Format: FASTA, or just click the FASTA link near the top of the record. FASTA is the simplest sequence format you'll meet.

    >NP_002046.1 glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase
MGKVKVGVNGFGRIGRLVTRAAFNSGKVDIVAINDPFIDLNYMVYMFQYDSTHGKFHGTVK
AENGKLVINGNPITIFQERDPSKIKWGDAGAEYVVESTGVFTTMEKAGAHLQGGAKRVII
SAPSADAPMFVMGVNHEKYDNSLKIISNASCTTNCLAPLAKVIHDNFGIVEGLMTTVHAI
...

    The first line (starting with >) is the header. The lines after it are the sequence. AssemblyBench will strip the header for you automatically when you paste.

  4. 4

    Decide: DNA or protein?

    AssemblyBench works with DNA (A, T, G, C). If you grabbed a protein sequence (which uses 20 letters like M, K, V, …), go back and find the coding DNA link on the same record. On UniProt, scroll to "Sequences" → "Cross-references" → click the EMBL/GenBank ID.

  5. 5

    Copy the sequence — not the annotations

    This is the #1 beginner mistake. Many records show the sequence with line numbers, capitalized features, and footnotes. Only copy the letters.

    • ✕ 1 atggtgagca agggcgagga gctgttcacc — has a number
    • ✕ >header line\nATG... if you keep the > in a tool that doesn't strip it
    • ✓ ATGGTGAGCAAGGGCGAGGAGCTGTTCACC... — clean, just letters

    AssemblyBench is forgiving: it strips numbers, spaces, newlines, FASTA headers, and non-ATGC characters automatically. But other tools won't be.

  6. 6

    Paste into AssemblyBench

    Open the Bench, click + Add a custom part (or pick an existing part on your track), and paste your sequence into the DNA sequence box. Give it a clear name like P_GAPDH or CDS_GFP.

One last thing. Real cloning has subtleties this site doesn't cover — codon optimization, restriction sites, reading frames, internal stop codons. AssemblyBench is a teaching tool: it gets you fluent in the vocabulary and logic of plasmid design so the real-world details click faster when you meet them.
