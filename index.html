What is a plasmid?

A plasmid is a small, circular piece of DNA that lives outside a cell's chromosome. Scientists use plasmids like USB sticks for cells — you load them with genetic instructions, hand them to a bacterium or yeast, and the cell carries them out.

Designing a plasmid is a bit like wiring a tiny circuit. Each part has a role, and the parts must be put in the right order for the cell to read them.

What is "assembly"?

Assembly is the process of stitching DNA parts together in a defined order to build a working plasmid. Modern methods (Golden Gate, Gibson, MoClo) snap parts together like LEGO bricks, each with matching connectors.

Why does it matter?

Plasmids are how we tell cells to make insulin, glow green, sense pollutants, produce biofuels, or test a gene's function. Every biotech product you've heard of started with somebody designing a plasmid.

The 6 parts every plasmid usually needs

Hover or tap a card to learn more. These are the pieces you'll snap together on the bench.

Promoter

The "on switch"

A DNA sequence that tells the cell "start reading here." Strong promoters make a lot of protein; weak ones make less.

RBS

Ribosome landing pad

The ribosome binding site tells the ribosome where to dock and start making protein from the mRNA.

CDS

The recipe

The coding sequence — the actual gene whose protein you want the cell to make (e.g., GFP, insulin).

Terminator

The "stop" sign

Tells the cell "you can stop transcribing now." Without it, the cell keeps reading into things it shouldn't.

Origin of Replication

How it copies itself

Where the cell starts duplicating the plasmid. Without an ori, the plasmid is lost when cells divide.

Resistance Marker

The "ID card"

An antibiotic-resistance gene (e.g., AmpR, KanR) that lets only cells with your plasmid survive on selective plates.

Step 1 — Research

How to find the part you need

Before you assemble, you need DNA sequences. Different databases are good for different things. Here's a beginner-friendly map of where to look.

Pub

PubMed

Find papers describing the part

Start here when you have a vague idea ("a fluorescent protein that's redder than mCherry"). Read the papers — they cite the original sequence sources.

Use when: you don't know what gene or organism yet.
G·S

Google Scholar

Broader literature search

Useful for grey literature, preprints, theses, and following citation trails. Pairs well with PubMed for early-stage discovery.

Use when: PubMed feels too narrow.
NCBI

NCBI (GenBank / Gene / Nucleotide)

The canonical DNA sequence source

Search by gene name, accession number, or organism. Most papers reference an NCBI accession — that's where the official DNA sequence lives.

Use when: you know the gene name and want the DNA.
Uni

UniProt

The protein-centric database

Best when you care about the protein (function, domains, structure links). Each UniProt entry links back to its coding DNA on NCBI.

Use when: you're picking a protein to express.
PDB

Protein Data Bank

3D protein structures

If you need to know what your protein looks like (active sites, binding pockets), the PDB is the place. Use it to decide which variant to clone.

Use when: you're engineering a protein, not just expressing it.
Add

Addgene

Real plasmids you can order

A non-profit plasmid repository. Search for a published plasmid, see its full sequence and map, and request it for ~$80. Excellent for backbones.

Use when: you want a known working plasmid as a starting point.
iGEM

iGEM Parts Registry

Community standard parts

Thousands of "BioBrick" parts (promoters, RBSs, reporters) submitted by student teams. Quality varies — check the experience pages.

Use when: you want common parts with prior characterization data.
Bench

Benchling Public

Shared cloning notebooks

Many labs publish their plasmid maps openly on Benchling. Searching here often turns up annotated sequences ready to copy.

Use when: you're hunting for a well-annotated map.
Beginner tip. If you're brand new, this is the typical path: idea → PubMed paper → NCBI accession → FASTA sequence → paste into AssemblyBench. Don't try to memorize all eight resources at once.

Step 2 — Sequence

How to grab the sequence

Once you know what you want, here's how to get the raw DNA letters into AssemblyBench without making the classic beginner mistakes.

  1. 1

    Search for your gene or protein

    On NCBI, use the Gene or Nucleotide database. On UniProt, use the search bar with the protein name plus the organism (e.g., GFP Aequorea victoria).

    Pro tip: include the organism. "Insulin" in human is a different sequence from "insulin" in zebrafish.

  2. 2

    Open the right record

    Pick the entry marked reviewed (on UniProt: a gold star) or the most-cited RefSeq (on NCBI: accession starts with NM_, NP_, or NC_).

  3. 3

    Switch the view to FASTA

    On NCBI, click "Send to" → File → Format: FASTA, or just click the FASTA link near the top of the record. FASTA is the simplest sequence format you'll meet.

    >NP_002046.1 glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase
MGKVKVGVNGFGRIGRLVTRAAFNSGKVDIVAINDPFIDLNYMVYMFQYDSTHGKFHGTVK
AENGKLVINGNPITIFQERDPSKIKWGDAGAEYVVESTGVFTTMEKAGAHLQGGAKRVII
SAPSADAPMFVMGVNHEKYDNSLKIISNASCTTNCLAPLAKVIHDNFGIVEGLMTTVHAI
...

    The first line (starting with >) is the header. The lines after it are the sequence. AssemblyBench will strip the header for you automatically when you paste.

  4. 4

    Decide: DNA or protein?

    AssemblyBench works with DNA (A, T, G, C). If you grabbed a protein sequence (which uses 20 letters like M, K, V, …), go back and find the coding DNA link on the same record. On UniProt, scroll to "Sequences" → "Cross-references" → click the EMBL/GenBank ID.

  5. 5

    Copy the sequence — not the annotations

    This is the #1 beginner mistake. Many records show the sequence with line numbers, capitalized features, and footnotes. Only copy the letters.

    • ✕ 1 atggtgagca agggcgagga gctgttcacc — has a number
    • ✕ >header line\nATG... if you keep the > in a tool that doesn't strip it
    • ✓ ATGGTGAGCAAGGGCGAGGAGCTGTTCACC... — clean, just letters

    AssemblyBench is forgiving: it strips numbers, spaces, newlines, FASTA headers, and non-ATGC characters automatically. But other tools won't be.

  6. 6

    Paste into AssemblyBench

    Open the Bench, click + Add a custom part (or pick an existing part on your track), and paste your sequence into the DNA sequence box. Give it a clear name like P_GAPDH or CDS_GFP.

One last thing. Real cloning has subtleties this site doesn't cover — codon optimization, restriction sites, reading frames, internal stop codons. AssemblyBench is a teaching tool: it gets you fluent in the vocabulary and logic of plasmid design so the real-world details click faster when you meet them.
