What is a plasmid?

A plasmid is a small, circular piece of DNA that lives outside a cell's chromosome. Scientists use plasmids like USB sticks for cells — you load them with genetic instructions, hand them to a bacterium or yeast, and the cell carries them out.

Designing a plasmid is a bit like wiring a tiny circuit. Each part has a role, and the parts must be put in the right order for the cell to read them.

What is "assembly"?

Assembly is the process of stitching DNA parts together in a defined order to build a working plasmid. Modern methods (Golden Gate, Gibson, MoClo) snap parts together like LEGO bricks, each with matching connectors.

Why does it matter?

Plasmids are how we tell cells to make insulin, glow green, sense pollutants, produce biofuels, or test a gene's function. Every biotech product you've heard of started with somebody designing a plasmid.

The 6 parts every plasmid usually needs

Hover or tap a card to learn more. These are the pieces you'll snap together on the bench.

Promoter

The "on switch"

A DNA sequence that tells the cell "start reading here." Strong promoters make a lot of protein; weak ones make less.

RBS

Ribosome landing pad

The ribosome binding site tells the ribosome where to dock and start making protein from the mRNA.

CDS

The recipe

The coding sequence — the actual gene whose protein you want the cell to make (e.g., GFP, insulin).

Terminator

The "stop" sign

Tells the cell "you can stop transcribing now." Without it, the cell keeps reading into things it shouldn't.

Origin of Replication

How it copies itself

Where the cell starts duplicating the plasmid. Without an ori, the plasmid is lost when cells divide.

Resistance Marker

The "ID card"

An antibiotic-resistance gene (e.g., AmpR, KanR) that lets only cells with your plasmid survive on selective plates.
