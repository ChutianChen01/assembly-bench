Golden Gate planner

This page turns the design from the Bench into a MoClo-style hierarchical Golden Gate cloning plan, following the approach in Lee et al., ACS Synth. Biol. 2015 (10.1021/sb500366v). Two Type IIS restriction enzymes are used at different tiers: BsaI for assembling parts into a cassette (transcription unit), and BsmBI for assembling cassettes into a multigene plasmid. Custom 4-bp fusion overhangs at each junction make the order deterministic.

Junction map

Each adjacent pair of parts is joined by a unique 4-bp fusion overhang. A red flag means an internal Type IIS site was found inside that part — it must be removed (silent codon change for CDSs) before Golden Gate will work.

Primer designs

Each part is PCR-amplified with primers that add a Type IIS recognition site, a spacer, and the 4-bp fusion overhang. The annealing region length is tuned per primer to land inside your target Tm window. Tm is the nearest-neighbor (SantaLucia 1998) Tm of the annealing portion at 250 nM primer + 50 mM Na⁺. Hairpin Tm is an estimate of the strongest self-complementary stem in the full primer — keep it >10 °C below your annealing temperature to avoid secondary structure.

Internal Type IIS site scan & domestication helper

How to remove internal sites (domestication workflow)
  1. Find every internal copy of the recognition site (forward and reverse strand) — the scan below does this automatically.
  2. Inside a CDS, change one of the codons that overlap the site to a synonymous codon, so the protein sequence is unchanged but the recognition motif is destroyed. The helper suggests an explicit base swap for each hit.
  3. Outside a CDS (promoter, RBS, terminator, origin, marker UTR), make the smallest single-base change that breaks the site while staying inside the functional region — favor changes in spacer regions over conserved motifs.
  4. Re-run the scan after every substitution to confirm both the chosen enzyme and any other Type IIS enzyme you may use downstream are clean.
  5. When the part is clean, click Apply suggested fix to this part to write the substitution back into your design.

Golden Gate fails if any part contains an internal recognition site for the chosen enzyme. This scan checks both strands and, for each hit, offers a silent / minimally invasive base swap that destroys the motif.

One-pot protocol

A typical Golden Gate reaction. Adjust enzyme units and equimolar input ratios for your specific kit (NEB Golden Gate Assembly Kit, Engler protocol, etc.).

Predicted assembled sequence

Concatenated post-ligation sequence, computed by stitching parts at their fusion overhangs. Use it to verify your design or as a reference for Sanger sequencing.
